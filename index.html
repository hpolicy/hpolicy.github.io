Professor of Health and Social Policy

Division of Health Policy & Management, Korea University
Director, Institute of Health Policy & Management, Korea University
International Faculty Affiliate, Centre for Global Social Policy, University of Toronto

I study the politics and policy of population health: how political choices and welfare-state institutions decide who gets to be healthy, and how health and social care can be redesigned to be more just. I work with multi-level and mixed methods to build the evidence that policy needs.

Trained first as a clinical pharmacist at Seoul National University and then in health and social policy, I earned my PhD at the Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health, served as a research scientist on the Employment Conditions Knowledge Network (EMCONET) of the WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health, and held a CIHR IHSPR Postdoctoral Fellowship in political science at the University of Toronto. My current work centres on smart healthy cities, health justice, and the comparative politics of health.

Research interests

What I work on

My research asks how macro-social forces — politics, welfare states, labour markets, and the design of cities — translate into the health of populations, and what evidence policy needs in order to change those outcomes.

Redesigning health & social care Health justice & equity Smart & healthy cities Comparative health & politics Program evaluation (causal inference) Systems & concept mapping
A conceptual model

Health is a political outcome

Policy has an address.

Health is produced by political choices about how a society organises its institutions. Political factors shape the welfare state, which takes form as labour market regulation and as the provision of health and social care; these act through mediating conditions — employment conditions and access to care — on the spatial distribution of risk that becomes population health and equity. Access works differently from the rest: it does not move the distribution of risk so much as govern how far that risk is translated into ill health, and so meets the pathway between risk and outcome — shifting it through social care, moderating it through health care. Above the whole field sits the Healthy City Atlas, a place-based layer of spatial data and analytics — a channel, not a cause — and the evidence it generates feeds back into policy.
